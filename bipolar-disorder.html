Overview

Those suffering from bipolar disorder experience drastic mood swings from emotional highs (mania) to emotional lows (depression). The rate at which these swings happen varies from person to person, but they commonly last from weeks to months.

In the manic state, you may feel euphoric, energetic, or easily irritable and become more talkative or overactive, while in the depressive state, you may feel symptoms such as sadness or hopelessness.

Symptoms

Mania and hypomania (less severe mania)
Symptoms of both mania and hypomania include:

  • Jumpy or sporadic nature
  • More active
  • More talkative
  • Impulsive decision making

Depression
Symptoms of the depressive state are similar to those of major depression. These include:

  • Feelings of sadness, hopelessness, and emptiness
  • Loss of motivation
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Loss of sex drive
  • Irritability
  • Fatigue and sleep changes

Treatments

Bipolar disorder is most commonly treated with some combination of medications and psychotherapy. The most popular medications are antidepressants, antipsychotics, and mood stabilizers. Psychotherapy is also an important form of treatment for this disorder. Types of psychotherapy used to treat bipolar disorder include cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) and interpersonal and social rhythm therapy.
